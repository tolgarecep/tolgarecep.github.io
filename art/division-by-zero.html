division by zero (chiang, 1991)
writer's cut

There is something that attracts me subliminally in events that test and reveal human condition which involve scientists; even when the main event and the scientific inquiry do not relate to each other on any level, scientists suffering through a tragedy is maybe the best way to understand humanity's edge of sense. Therea are many great works which deal with human condition with sci-fi elements, but here I talk about a style in which science is primarily irrelevant than usual. In literary, Ted Chiang did two works which I find relative to this genre, one of them adapted to screen with the title Arrival (Villeneuve, 2016). After I read his work, I started to develop this aspiration for such narrative, another work fullfilled it was As She Climbed Across the Table by Jonathan Lethem.

Division by Zero is a short story about a mathematician who develops a formalism that proves the arithmetic to be totally irrelevant to physical world, by it's nature that enables you to equate any two number you like. It is a story harmonising relationships, science, and search for meaning. As the main character deals with unrevealing of such giant collapse of trust in tools made her life mean something, she becomes someone hard to empathisize with, and this puts status quo in danger: every character, even those who are just present in thoughts of main characters, suffer through a transmutation. Every now and then it feels like the insidious agonies in this story would be done justice through cinema, which is the feeling that lead me to find a short film, Gödel Incomplete (Martha Goddard, 2014), telling about an accident of a physicist which lets her time-travel and meet Kurt Gödel to discuss his theorems and time-travel. Not what I had been searching, but another installment with decent execution to our list.

Although I feel like it overflows from the boundaries of the genre I feel that exists, I must talk a bit about Contact, a novel by Carl Sagan also became a film by Robert Zemeckis. It feels out of the boundary since what I'm talking about here is not sci-fi that cares about people in it, but specific dramas that care about sci-fi. I love Contact because of its way of dealing with science ethic, which was an introduction to being a scientist for me when I read it 15 years old. It is an essential work laying out the beauty of the culture of doubt.
